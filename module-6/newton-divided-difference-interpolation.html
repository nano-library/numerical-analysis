✨ Newton's divided difference method is often preferred due to its incremental nature. Unlike Lagrange interpolation, which requires recomputing the entire polynomial when adding new data, Newton's method builds on previous calculations, making it ideal for sequential or real-time data. It also offers better insight through divided differences, which resemble derivatives, and is more efficient and flexible for large datasets.
Given a set of \(n + 1\) distinct data points \((x_0, y_0), (x_1, y_1), \ldots , (x_n, y_n)\),
where \(x_0 < x_1 < \ldots < x_n\), the Newton divided difference (DD) polynomial is expressed as:
✦
\(P(x)\) \(=\) \(f(x_0)\) \(+\) \((x - x_0)\) \(f[x_0, x_1]\)
\(+\) \((x - x_0)\) \((x - x_1)\) \(f[x_0, x_1, x_2]\)
\(+\) \(\cdots\)
\(+\) \((x - x_0)\) \((x - x_1)\) \(\cdots\) \((x - x_{n-1})\) \(f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n]\)
where \(f[x_i, \ldots , x_j]\) denotes the divided differences, defined recursively by,
for \(i = 0, 1, \ldots, n\),
✦
\(f[x_i]\) \(=\) \(f(x_i),\)
✦
\(f[x_i, x_{i+1}, \ldots, x_{i+k}]\) \(=\) \(f[x_{i+1}, \ldots, x_{i+k}]\) \(-\) \(f[x_i, \ldots, x_{i+k-1}]\) \(x_{i+k}\) \(-\) \(x_i\)
\(x\) \(f(x)\) First DD Second DD
\(x_0\) \(f[x_0]\)
\(f[x_0, x_1]\)
\(x_1\) \(f[x_1]\) \(f[x_0, x_1, x_2]\)
\(f[x_1, x_2]\)
\(x_2\) \(f[x_2]\)
For distinct points \(x_0, x_1, \ldots , x_n\), where \(w(x) = \displaystyle\prod_{j=0}^n (x - x_j)\),
✦
\(f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_k]\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n}\) \(f(x_i)\) \(\displaystyle\prod_{j=0,\, j\neq i}^{n}(x_i - x_j)\)
✦
\(f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_k]\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{i=0}^{n}\) \(f(x_i)\) \(w'(x_i)\)
✦ Example 1 constructing newton divided difference polynomial
Problem
Consider the data points (1, 2), (3, 5), (4, 6), and (5, 8). We aim to construct the Newton divided difference polynomial and approximate the value at \(x = 2\).
Solution

Step 1: Construct the Divided Difference Table

Using the given points, we compute the divided differences as follows:

\[ \begin{array}{ccccc} x & f(x) & \text{First DD} & \text{Second DD} & \text{Third DD} \\ \hline 1 & 2 & & & \\ & & \dfrac{5-2}{3-1} = \dfrac{3}{2} & & \\ 3 & 5 & & \dfrac{1-\frac{3}{2}}{4-1} = -\dfrac{1}{6} & \\ & & \dfrac{6-5}{4-3} = 1 & & \dfrac{\frac{1}{2}-\left(-\frac{1}{6}\right)}{5-1} = \dfrac{1}{6} \\ 4 & 6 & & \dfrac{2-1}{5-3} = \dfrac{1}{2} & \\ & & \dfrac{8-6}{5-4} = 2 & & \\ 5 & 8 & & & \\ \end{array} \]

Step 2: Form the Polynomial

\[ \begin{aligned} P(x) = \; &f(x_0) + (x - x_0)f[x_0, x_1] \\ &+ (x - x_0)(x - x_1)f[x_0, x_1, x_2] \\ &+ \cdots \\ &+ (x - x_0)(x - x_1) \cdots (x - x_{n-1})f[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n], \end{aligned} \]

The interpolating polynomial is:

\[ \begin{aligned} P(x) = \; & 2 + (x - 1)(\frac{3}{2}) \\ &+ (x - 1)(x - 3)(-\frac{1}{6}) \\ &+ (x - 1)(x - 3)(x - 4)(\frac{1}{6}) \end{aligned} \]

Step 3: Substitute \(x = 2\):

\[ \begin{aligned} P(2) = \; & 2 + (2 - 1)(\frac{3}{2}) \\ &+ (2 - 1)(2 - 3)(-\frac{1}{6}) \\ &+ (2 - 1)(2 - 3)(2 - 4)(\frac{1}{6}) \end{aligned} \] \[ \begin{aligned} P(2) = \; & 2 + (1)(1.5) \\ &+ (1)(-1)(-0.1666) \\ &+ (1)(-1)(-2)(0.1666) \end{aligned} \] \[ P(2) = 2 + 1.5 + 0.1666 + 0.3332 \]

Therefore,

\[ P(2) \approx 3.9998 \]
