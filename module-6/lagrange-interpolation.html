✨ Interpolation is a mathematical and data analysis method used to estimate unknown values that fall between known data points. Lagrange interpolation provides a unique polynomial interpolant in a straightforward form.
Given \(n + 1\) data points \((x_0, y_0), (x_1, y_1), \ldots, (x_n, y_n)\), we seek a polynomial \(L(x)\) of degree at most \(n\) such that, for \(i = 0, 1, \ldots , n\),
✦
\(L(x_i)\) \(=\) \(y_i\)
Lagrange interpolation provides an explicit formula for this polynomial.

Derivation of the lagrange interpolating polynomial

We seek a polynomial \(L(x)\) of degree at most n that satisfies the interpolation conditions, for \(i = 0, 1, \ldots , n\),
✦
\(L(x_i)\) \(=\) \(y_i\)
To construct \(L(x)\), we first define polynomials \(\ell_j(x)\) such that each \(\ell_j(x)\) satisfies:
✦
\( {\color{#6AAEE0}{\ell_j(x_i)}} {\color{#F08FB0}{=}} \begin{cases} {\color{#6AAEE0}{1}}, & \text{if } {\color{#6AAEE0}{i}} {\color{#F08FB0}{=}} {\color{#6AAEE0}{j}} \\ {\color{#6AAEE0}{0}}, & \text{if } {\color{#6AAEE0}{i}} {\color{#F08FB0}{\neq}} {\color{#6AAEE0}{j}} \end{cases} \)
A polynomial of degree \(n\) that vanishes at all points \(x_m\) except at \(x_j\) can be written as:
✦
\(\ell_j(x)\) \(=\) \(C_j\) \(\displaystyle\prod_{{m=0\\ m \neq j}}^n\) \((\) \(x\) \(-\) \(x_m\) \()\)
To ensure \(\ell_j(x_j) = 1\), we set
✦
\(C_j\) \(=\) \(1\) \(\displaystyle\prod_{{m=0\\ m \neq j}}^n\) \((\) \(x_j\) \(-\) \(x_m\) \()\)
Thus, the Lagrange basis polynomials take the form:
✦
\(\ell_j(x)\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\prod_{{m=0\\ m \neq j}}^n\) \((\) \(x\) \(-\) \(x_m\) \()\) \((\) \(x_j\) \(-\) \(x_m\) \()\)
Since \(L(x)\) must interpolate the given data, we express it as a linear combination:
✦
\(L(x)\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^n\) \(a_j \ell_j(x)\)
Imposing the interpolation conditions \(L(x_i) = y_i\), we get:
✦
\(y_i\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^n\) \(a_j \ell_j(x_i)\)
Due to the Kronecker delta property \(\ell_j(x_i) = \delta_{ij}\), this simplifies to:
✦
\(y_i\) \(=\) \(a_i\)
Thus, we obtain the explicit Lagrange interpolation formula:
✦
\(L(x)\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\sum_{j=0}^n\) \(y_j \ell_j(x)\)
where
\(\ell_j(x)\) \(=\) \(\displaystyle\prod_{m=0\\ m \neq j}^n \frac{x - x_m}{x_j - x_m}\)
✦ Example 1 lagrange interpolating polynomial
Problem

Consider the points (1, 2), (2, 3), and (3, 5). The corresponding Lagrange interpolating polynomial is:

Solution

Lagrange interpolating polynomial definition:

\[ L(x) = \sum_{j=0}^n y_j \ell_j(x) \]

where,

\[ \ell_j (x) = \prod_{m=0\\ m \neq j}^n \frac{x - x_m}{x_j - x_m} \]

We get,

\[ L(x) = 2\ell_1(x) + 3\ell_2(x) + 5\ell_3(x) \]

Solving,

\[ \ell_1(x) = \frac{(x-2)(x-3)}{(1-2)(1-3)} \\[10pt] \ell_2(x) = \frac{(x-1)(x-3)}{(2-1)(2-3)} \\[10pt] \ell_3(x) = \frac{(x-1)(x-2)}{(3-1)(3-2)} \]

Simplifing,

\[ \ell_1(x) = \frac{x^2-3x-2x+6}{2} \\[10pt] \ell_2(x) = \frac{x^2-3x-x+3}{-1} \\[10pt] \ell_3(x) = \frac{x^2-2x-x+2}{2} \]

Lagrange interpolating polynomial is:

\[ \begin{aligned} L(x) = \; & 2\left(\frac{x^2-5x+6}{2}\right) \\ &+ 3\left(\frac{x^2-4x+3}{-1}\right) \\ &+ 5\left(\frac{x^2-3x+2}{2}\right) \\ \end{aligned} \]
✦ Example 2 lagrange interpolating polynomial
Problem

Given the data points (0, 1), (1, 2), and (2, 0), determine the Lagrange interpolating polynomial explicitly and simplify your result.

Solution

Lagrange interpolating polynomial definition:

\[ L(x) = \sum_{j=0}^n y_j \ell_j(x) \]

where,

\[ \ell_j (x) = \prod_{m=0\\ m \neq j}^n \frac{x - x_m}{x_j - x_m} \]

We get,

\[ L(x) = 1\ell_1(x) + 2\ell_2(x) + 0\ell_3(x) \]

Solving,

\[ \ell_1(x) = \frac{(x-1)(x-2)}{(0-1)(0-2)} \\[10pt] \ell_2(x) = \frac{(x-0)(x-2)}{(1-0)(1-2)} \\[10pt] \ell_3(x) = \frac{(x-0)(x-1)}{(2-0)(2-1)} \\ \]

Simplifing,

\[ \ell_1(x) = \frac{x^2-3x+2}{2} \\[10pt] \ell_2(x) = \frac{x^2 - 2x}{-1} \\[10pt] \ell_3(x) = \frac{x^2 - x}{2} \]

Lagrange interpolating polynomial is:

\[ \begin{aligned} L(x) = \; & \left(\frac{x^2-3x+2}{2}\right) \\ &+ 2\left(-x^2 + 2x\right) \\ &+ 0\left(\frac{x^2 - x}{2}\right) \end{aligned} \]
