✨ To factor the positive definite \(n \times n\) matrix \(A\) into \(LL^T\), where \(L\) is lower triangular.
🌸

If A is real symmetric matrix and positive definite matrix, then it has a unique factorisation \(A = LL^T\), in which \(L\) is lower triangular with a positive diagonal.

✦ Example 1 cholesky factorization of matrix
Problem

Find the Cholesky factorization of the following matrix:

\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 2\\ 2 & 5 & 1\\ 2 & 1 & 6 \end{bmatrix}

Check whether all the principal minors are positive before proceeding with the factorization.

Solution

For a symmetric matrix A to have a Cholesky factorization, it must be positive definite, which means all its principal minors must be positive.

Step 1: Check principal minors

The leading principal minors are

\[ D_1 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 \end{vmatrix} = 4 > 0 \] \[ D_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 2\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_2 = |20 - 4| = 16 > 0 \] \[ D_3 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 2 & 2\\ 2 & 5 & 1\\ 2 & 1 & 6 \end{vmatrix} \] \[ \small D_3 = (4) \begin{vmatrix} 5 & 1\\ 1 & 6\\ \end{vmatrix} - (2) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 1\\ 2 & 6\\ \end{vmatrix} + (2) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 5\\ 2 & 1\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_3 = 4 (30 - 1) - 2 (12 - 2) + 2 (2 - 10)\\[10pt] D_3 = 4(29) - 2(10) + 2(-8)\\[10pt] D_3 = 116 - 20 - 8\\[10pt] D_3 = 80 > 0 \]

Since all principal minors are positive, \(A\) is positive definite, and we can proceed with the Cholesky factorization.

Step 2: Assume lower triangular matrix \(L\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21} & l_{22} & 0\\ l_{31} & l_{32} & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & l_{21} & l_{31}\\ 0 & l_{22} & l_{32}\\ 0 & 0 & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \small LL^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A = LL^T \] \[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^2 &= 4 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{21} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{31} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{21}^2 + l_{22}^2 &= 5 \\[10pt] l_{21}l_{31} + l_{22}l_{33} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{31}^2 + l_{32}^2 + l_{33}^2 &= 6 \end{aligned} \]

Solving the equations, we get

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{21} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{22} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{31} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{32} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= \sqrt{5} \end{aligned} \]

We obtain \(L\) and \(L^T\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 2 & 0\\ 1 & 0 & \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1\\ 0 & 2 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix} \]

Thus, the Cholesky factorization of \(A\) is

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 2 & 0\\ 1 & 0 & \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1\\ 0 & 2 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{5} \end{bmatrix} \]
✦ Example 2 cholesky factorization of matrix
Problem

Find the inverse of the matrix using the Cholesky method:

\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 2\\ -1 & 1 & -1\\ 2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}
Solution

For a symmetric matrix A to have a Cholesky factorization, it must be positive definite, which means all its principal minors must be positive.

Step 1: Check principal minors

The leading principal minors are

\[ D_1 = \begin{vmatrix} 2 \end{vmatrix} = 2 > 0 \] \[ D_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 2 & -1\\ -1 & 1\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_2 = |2 - 1| = 2 > 0 \] \[ D_3 = \begin{vmatrix} 2 & -1 & 2\\ -1 & 1 & -1\\ 2 & -1 & 3 \end{vmatrix} \] \[ \small D_3 = (2) \begin{vmatrix} 1 & -1\\ -1 & 3\\ \end{vmatrix} - (-1) \begin{vmatrix} -1 & -1\\ 2 & 3\\ \end{vmatrix} + (2) \begin{vmatrix} -1 & 1\\ 2 & -1\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_3 = 2 (3 - 1) + 1 (-3 + 2) + 2 (1 - 2)\\[10pt] D_3 = 2(2) + 1(-1) + 2(-1)\\[10pt] D_3 = 4 - 1 - 2\\[10pt] D_3 = 1 > 0 \]

Since all principal minors are positive, \(A\) is positive definite, and we can proceed with the Cholesky factorization.

Step 2: Assume lower triangular matrix \(L\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21} & l_{22} & 0\\ l_{31} & l_{32} & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & l_{21} & l_{31}\\ 0 & l_{22} & l_{32}\\ 0 & 0 & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \small LL^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A = LL^T \] \[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^2 &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{21} &= -1 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{31} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{21}^2 + l_{22}^2 &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{21}l_{31} + l_{22}l_{33} &= -1 \\[10pt] l_{31}^2 + l_{32}^2 + l_{33}^2 &= 3 \end{aligned} \]

Solving the equations, we get

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11} &= \sqrt{2} \\[10pt] l_{21} &= \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} \\[10pt] l_{22} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\[10pt] l_{31} &= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} \\[10pt] l_{32} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

We obtain \(L\) and \(L^T\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{2} & 0 & 0\\ \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0\\ \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Since \(L^{-1}\) is also a lower triangular matrix, we solve for it:

\[ LL^{-1} = I \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{2} & 0 & 0\\ \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0\\ \sqrt{2} & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^{*} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21}^{*} & l_{22}^{*} & 0\\ l_{31}^{*} & l_{32}^{*} & l_{33}^{*} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Solving,

\[ \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{2}l_{11}^{*} & 0 & 0\\ \frac{-l_{11}^{*}+l_{21}^{*}}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{l_{22}^{*}}{\sqrt{2}} & 0\\ \sqrt{2}l_{11}^{*}+l_{31}^{*} & l_{32}^{*} & l_{33}^{*} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{aligned} \sqrt{2}l_{11}^{*} &= 1 \\[10pt] \frac{-l_{11}^{*}+l_{21}^{*}}{\sqrt{2}} &= 0 \\[10pt] \frac{l_{22}^{*}}{\sqrt{2}} &= 1 \\[10pt] \sqrt{2}l_{11}^{*}+l_{31}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{32}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

we get,

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^{*} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\[10pt] l_{21}^{*} &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \\[10pt] l_{22}^{*} &= \sqrt{2} \\[10pt] l_{31}^{*} &= -1 \\[10pt] l_{32}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33}^{*} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

Now, \(L^{-1}\) can be written as

\[ L^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & 0\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \sqrt{2} & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

For \(A^{-1}\),

\[ A^{-1} = (LL^T)^{-1} = (L^T)^{-1}(L)^{-1} = (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} \] \[ (L^{-1})^T = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -1\\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -1\\ 0 & \sqrt{2} & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 & 0\\ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \sqrt{2} & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1\\ 1 & 2 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A^{-1} = (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} \]

Thus, the inverse of \(A\) is:

\[ A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1\\ 1 & 2 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]
✦ Example 3 cholesky factorization of matrix
Problem

Find the Cholesky factorization of the following matrix:

\begin{bmatrix} 25 & 15 & -5\\ 15 & 18 & 0\\ -5 & 0 & 11 \end{bmatrix}

Check whether all the principal minors are positive before proceeding with the factorization.

Solution

For a symmetric matrix A to have a Cholesky factorization, it must be positive definite, which means all its principal minors must be positive.

Step 1: Check principal minors

The leading principal minors are

\[ D_1 = \begin{vmatrix} 25 \end{vmatrix} = 25 > 0 \] \[ D_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 25 & 15\\ 15 & 18\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_2 = |450 - 225| = 225 > 0 \] \[ D_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 25 & 15 & -5\\ 15 & 18 & 0\\ -5 & 0 & 11 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \small D_3 = (25) \begin{vmatrix} 18 & 0\\ 0 & 11\\ \end{vmatrix} - (15) \begin{vmatrix} 15 & 0\\ -5 & 11\\ \end{vmatrix} + (-5) \begin{vmatrix} 15 & 18\\ -5 & 0\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_3 = 25 (198 - 0) - 15 (165 - 0) - 5 (0 + 90)\\[10pt] D_3 = 25(198) - 15(165) - 5(90)\\[10pt] D_3 = 4950 - 2475 - 450\\[10pt] D_3 = 2025 > 0 \]

Since all principal minors are positive, \(A\) is positive definite, and we can proceed with the Cholesky factorization.

Step 2: Assume lower triangular matrix \(L\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21} & l_{22} & 0\\ l_{31} & l_{32} & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & l_{21} & l_{31}\\ 0 & l_{22} & l_{32}\\ 0 & 0 & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \small LL^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A = LL^T \] \[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^2 &= 25 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{21} &= 15 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{31} &= -5 \\[10pt] l_{21}^2 + l_{22}^2 &= 18 \\[10pt] l_{21}l_{31} + l_{22}l_{32} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{31}^2 + l_{32}^2 + l_{33}^2 &= 11 \end{aligned} \]

Solving the equations, we get

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11} &= 5 \\[10pt] l_{21} &= 3 \\[10pt] l_{22} &= 3 \\[10pt] l_{31} &= -1 \\[10pt] l_{32} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= 3 \end{aligned} \]

We obtain \(L\) and \(L^T\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 & 0\\ 3 & 3 & 0\\ -1 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 & -1\\ 0 & 3 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Thus, the Cholesky factorization of \(A\) is

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 & 0\\ 3 & 3 & 0\\ -1 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 3 & -1\\ 0 & 3 & 1\\ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \]
✦ Example 4 cholesky factorization of matrix
Problem

Find the inverse of the matrix using the Cholesky method:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 4\\ 2 & 2 & 2\\ 4 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \]
Solution

For a symmetric matrix A to have a Cholesky factorization, it must be positive definite, which means all its principal minors must be positive.

Step 1: Check principal minors

The leading principal minors are

\[ D_1 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 \end{vmatrix} = 4 > 0 \] \[ D_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 2\\ 2 & 2\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_2 = |8 - 4| = 4 > 0 \] \[ D_3 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 2 & 4\\ 2 & 2 & 2\\ 4 & 2 & 5 \end{vmatrix} \] \[ \small D_3 = (4) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 2\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{vmatrix} - (2) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 2\\ 4 & 5\\ \end{vmatrix} + (4) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 2\\ 4 & 2\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_3 = 4(10 - 4) - 2(10 - 8) + 4 (4 - 8)\\[10pt] D_3 = 4(6) - 2(2) + 4(-4)\\[10pt] D_3 = 24 - 4 - 16\\[10pt] D_3 = 4 > 0 \]

Since all principal minors are positive, \(A\) is positive definite, and we can proceed with the Cholesky factorization.

Step 2: Assume lower triangular matrix \(L\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21} & l_{22} & 0\\ l_{31} & l_{32} & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & l_{21} & l_{31}\\ 0 & l_{22} & l_{32}\\ 0 & 0 & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \small LL^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A = LL^T \] \[ \tiny \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 4\\ 2 & 2 & 2\\ 4 & 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^2 &= 4 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{21} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{31} &= 4 \\[10pt] l_{21}^2 + l_{22}^2 &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{21}l_{31} + l_{22}l_{33} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{31}^2 + l_{32}^2 + l_{33}^2 &= 5 \end{aligned} \]

Solving the equations, we get

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{21} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{22} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{31} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{32} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

We obtain \(L\) and \(L^T\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0\\ 2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Since \(L^{-1}\) is also a lower triangular matrix, we solve for it:

\[ LL^{-1} = I \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 1 & 0\\ 2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^{*} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21}^{*} & l_{22}^{*} & 0\\ l_{31}^{*} & l_{32}^{*} & l_{33}^{*} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Solving,

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 2l_{11}^{*} & 0 & 0\\ l_{11}^{*}+l_{21}^{*} & l_{22}^{*} & 0\\ 2l_{11}^{*}+l_{31}^{*} & l_{32}^{*} & l_{33}^{*} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{aligned} 2l_{11}^{*} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{11}^{*}+l_{21}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{22}^{*} &= 1 \\[10pt] 2l_{11}^{*}+l_{31}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{32}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

we get,

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^{*} &= \frac{1}{2} \\[10pt] l_{21}^{*} &= -\frac{1}{2} \\[10pt] l_{22}^{*} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{31}^{*} &= -1 \\[10pt] l_{32}^{*} &= 0 \\[10pt] l_{33}^{*} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

Now, \(L^{-1}\) can be written as

\[ L^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0\\ -\frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

For \(A^{-1}\),

\[ A^{-1} = (LL^T)^{-1} = (L^T)^{-1}(L)^{-1} = (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} \] \[ (L^{-1})^T = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} & -1\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & 0 & 0\\ -\frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} & -1\\ 0 & 1 & 0\\ 0 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{3}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} & -1\\ -\frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A^{-1} = (L^{-1})^TL^{-1} \]

Thus, the inverse of \(A\) is:

\[ A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{3}{2} & -\frac{1}{2} & -1\\ -\frac{1}{2} & 1 & 0\\ -1 & 0 & 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]
✦ Example 5 solving system of equations
Problem

Solve the following system of equations by Cholesky's method

\[ 4x+2y+6z=16 \\[10pt] 2x+82y+39z=206\\[10pt] 6x+39y+26z=113\\[10pt] \]
Solution

The given system of equations can be written in the matrix Form \(AX = B\)

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 6\\ 2 & 82 & 39\\ 6 & 39 & 26 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} z \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 16 \\ 206 \\ 113 \end{bmatrix} \]

Here,

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 6\\ 2 & 82 & 39\\ 6 & 39 & 26 \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] X = \begin{bmatrix} z \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] B = \begin{bmatrix} 16 \\ 206 \\ 113 \end{bmatrix} \]

Verify \(A\) is Symmetric Positive Definite

For symmetric

\[ A = A^T \] \[ A^T = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 6\\ 2 & 82 & 39\\ 6 & 39 & 26 \end{bmatrix} \]

\(A\) is symmetric

For a symmetric matrix A to have a Cholesky factorization, it must be positive definite, which means all its principal minors must be positive.

The leading principal minors are

\[ D_1 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 \end{vmatrix} = 4 > 0 \] \[ D_2 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 2\\ 2 & 82\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_2 = |328 - 4| = 324 > 0 \] \[ D_3 = \begin{vmatrix} 4 & 2 & 6\\ 2 & 82 & 39\\ 6 & 39 & 26 \end{vmatrix} \] \[ \small D_3 = (4) \begin{vmatrix} 82 & 39\\ 39 & 26\\ \end{vmatrix} - (2) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 39\\ 6 & 26\\ \end{vmatrix} + (6) \begin{vmatrix} 2 & 82\\ 6 & 39\\ \end{vmatrix} \] \[ D_3 = 4(2132 - 1521) - 2(52 - 234) + 6 (78 - 492)\\[10pt] D_3 = 4(611) - 2(-182) + 6(-414)\\[10pt] D_3 = 2444 + 364 - 2484\\[10pt] D_3 = 324 > 0 \]

Since all principal minors are positive, \(A\) is positive definite, and we can proceed with the Cholesky factorization.

Step 2: Assume lower triangular matrix \(L\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & 0 & 0\\ l_{21} & l_{22} & 0\\ l_{31} & l_{32} & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ L^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11} & l_{21} & l_{31}\\ 0 & l_{22} & l_{32}\\ 0 & 0 & l_{33} \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \small LL^T = \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A = LL^T \] \[ \tiny \begin{bmatrix} l_{11}^2 & l_{11}l_{21} & l_{11}l_{31}\\ l_{11}l_{21} & l_{21}^2+l_{22}^2 & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32}\\ l_{11}l_{31} & l_{21}l_{31}+l_{22}l_{32} & l_{31}^2+l_{32}^2+l_{33}^2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & 6\\ 2 & 82 & 39\\ 6 & 39 & 26 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{aligned} l_{11}^2 &= 4 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{21} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{11}l_{31} &= 6 \\[10pt] l_{21}^2 + l_{22}^2 &= 82 \\[10pt] l_{21}l_{31} + l_{22}l_{32} &= 39 \\[10pt] l_{31}^2 + l_{32}^2 + l_{33}^2 &= 26 \end{aligned} \]

Solving the equations, we get

\[ \begin{aligned} l_{11} &= 2 \\[10pt] l_{21} &= 1 \\[10pt] l_{22} &= 9 \\[10pt] l_{31} &= 3 \\[10pt] l_{32} &= 4 \\[10pt] l_{33} &= 1 \end{aligned} \]

We obtain \(L\) and \(L^T\)

\[ L = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 9 & 0\\ 3 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Solve \(LY = B\) (Forward Substitution)

Since,

\[ AX = B \\[10pt] LL^TX = B \]

Let \(L^TX = Y\), we solve

\[ LY = B \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0\\ 1 & 9 & 0\\ 3 & 4 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ y_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 16 \\ 206 \\ 113 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ 2y_1 = 16 \\[10pt] y_1 + 2y_2 = 206 \\[10pt] 2y_1 + 4y_2 + y_3 = 113 \]

After solving, we get

\[ Y = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 22 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Solve \(L^TX = Y\) (Back Substitution)

\[ L^TX = Y \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3\\ 0 & 9 & 4\\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 22 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ 2x + y + 3z = 8 \\[10pt] 9y + 4z = 22 \\[10pt] z = 1 \]

Thus,

\[ x = \frac{3}{2} \\[10pt] y = 2 \\[10pt] z = 1 \]
