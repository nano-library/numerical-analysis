The eigenvalues of \(A^{-1}\) are the reciprocal of the eigenvalues of A. Therefore, the reciprocal of the dominating eigenvalue of \(A^{-1}\) is the smallest eigenvalue of A. The following iteration formula finds the dominating eigenvector of \(A^{-1}\), \(k = 1,2,\ldots\),
✦ Inverse Power Method
✦
\(y^{(k)}\) = \(A^{-1} v^{(k-1)}\)
✦ Inverse Power Method
✦
\(\alpha^{(k)}\) = \(\|y^{(k)}\|_{\infty}\)
✦ Inverse Power Method
✦
\(v^{(k)}\) = \(y^{(k)}\) \(\|y^{(k)}\|_{\infty}\)
We do not calculate the inverse in practice, instead we solve the system \(Ay^{(k)} = v^{(k - 1)}\) to get \(y^{(k)}\). One can apply LU decomposition here. As eigenvector of A and \(A^{-1}\) remains the same we only have to find the associated eigenvalue of A using
✦
\(\lambda^{(k)}_{\text{min}}\) = 1 \(\alpha^{(k)}\)
💠
Note
For the convergence of inverse power method, we require below equation,
✦
\(|\lambda_1|\) > \(|\lambda_2|\) \(\geq\) \(|\lambda_3|\) \(\geq\) \(\ldots\) \(\geq\) \(|\lambda_{n-1}|\) > \(|\lambda_n|\)
✦ Example 2 finding the smallest eigenvalue
Problem

Consider the following \(2 \times 2\) matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1\\ 1 & 2\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Use the inverse power method to find the smallest eigenvalue in magnitude and its corresponding eigenvector.

Initial guess:

\[ v^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
Solution

First compute \(A^{-1}\) for a \(2\times2\) matrix:

\[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \begin{bmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \det(A) = (3)(2) - (1)(1) = 6 - 1 = 5 \] \[ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ A^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & -0.2 \\ -0.2 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 1:

\[ y^{(1)} = A^{-1} v^{(0)} \] \[ y^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & -0.2 \\ -0.2 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.2 \\ 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \alpha^{(1)} = \|y^{(1)}\|_{\infty} = \max(|0.2|, |0.4|) = 0.4 \] \[ \lambda^{(1)} = \frac{1}{\alpha^{(1)}} = \frac{1}{0.4} = 2.5 \] \[ v^{(1)} = \frac{y^{(1)}}{\alpha^{(1)}} \\[10pt] v^{(1)} = \frac{1}{0.4} \begin{bmatrix} 0.2 \\ 0.4 \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Scaling so first component = 1

\[ v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 2:

\[ y^{(2)} = A^{-1} v^{(1)} \] \[ y^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & -0.2 \\ -0.2 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0.5 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \alpha^{(2)} = \|y^{(2)}\|_{\infty} = \max(|0|, |0.5|) = 0.5 \] \[ \lambda^{(2)} = \frac{1}{\alpha^{(2)}} = \frac{1}{0.5} = 2 \] \[ v^{(2)} = \frac{y^{(1)}}{\alpha^{(1)}} \\[10pt] v^{(2)} = \frac{1}{0.5} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 3:

\[ y^{(3)} = A^{-1} v^{(2)} \] \[ y^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.4 & -0.2 \\ -0.2 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -0.2 \\ 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \alpha^{(3)} = \|y^{(3)}\|_{\infty} = \max(|-0.2|, |0.6|) = 0.6 \] \[ \lambda^{(3)} = \frac{1}{\alpha^{(3)}} = \frac{1}{0.6} = 1.6666 \] \[ v^{(3)} = \frac{y^{(1)}}{\alpha^{(1)}} \\[10pt] v^{(3)} = \frac{1}{0.6} \begin{bmatrix} -0.2 \\ 0.6 \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} -\frac{1}{3} \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Scaling so first component = 1

\[ v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \]

Eigenvalues of A:

\[ |A - \lambda I| = 0 \] \[ (3-\lambda)(2-\lambda) - 1 = 0 \\[10pt] \lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 6 - 1 = 0\\[10pt] \lambda^2 - 5\lambda + 5 = 0 \] \[ \lambda_1 \approx 3.6180, \quad \lambda_2 \approx 1.3820 \]

The smallest eigenvalue in magnitude is \(\lambda_{\min} \approx 1.3820\).
Eigenvector for \(\lambda_{\min}\):

\[ (A - \lambda_{\min} I)x = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 3 - 1.3820 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 - 1.3820 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1.6181 & 1 \\ 1 & 0.6181 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \]

From first row: \(1.6180 x_1 + x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2 = -1.6181 x_1\).
Let \(x_1 = 1\), then \(x_2 = -1.6181\).
Normalized dominant eigenvector (first component 1): \[ v_{\min} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -1.6181 \end{bmatrix} \] The inverse power method with \(y^{(k+1)} = A^{-1}v^{(k)}\) yields:

\(\lambda^{(1)} = 2.5\), \(v^{(1)} = [1, 2]^T\)

\(\lambda^{(2)} = 2.0\), \(v^{(2)} = [0, 1]^T\)

\(\lambda^{(3)} = 1.6666\), \(v^{(3)} = [1, -3]^T\).
The approximations converge to the exact smallest eigenvalue \(\lambda_{\min} \approx 1.3820\) and eigenvector \([1, -1.6180]^T\).
Final approximation after 3 iterations:

\[ \lambda_{\min} \approx 1.6666, \qquad v_{\min} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} \]
