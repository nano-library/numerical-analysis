✨ This approach approximates the dominant eigenvalue and the associated eigenvector for a given n-dimensional matrix A.
We make the following assumptions regarding the matrix A.
  • There exists a unique eigenvalue with the maximum absolute value.
  • The matrix possesses a complete set of n linearly independent eigenvectors.
From the first assumption, we have n eigenvalues \(\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n\) with
✦
\(|\lambda_1|\) > \(|\lambda_2|\) \(\geq\) \(|\lambda_3|\) \(\geq\) \(\ldots\) \(\geq\) \(|\lambda_n|\)
From the second assumption let us assume the associated eigenvectors are \(x_i\;(i = 1, 2 \ldots n)\). Thus \(\lambda_1\) is the dominating eigenvalue of A with associated eigenvector \(x_1\).
Suppose \(v^{(0)}\) is the initial guess. Then \(v^{(0)}\) can be written as
✦
\(v^{(0)}\) = \(\alpha_1 x_1\) + \(\alpha_2 x_2\) + \(\ldots\) + \(\alpha_n x_n\)
We assume, \(\alpha_1 \neq 0\). We then iteratively find,
✦
\(v^{(1)}\) = \(A v^{(0)}\)
✦
\(v^{(2)}\) = \(A v^{(1)}\) = \(A A v^{(0)}\) = \(A^2 v^{(0)}\)
✦
\(v^{(k)}\) = \(A^k v^{(0)}\)
Now, where \(\epsilon^{(k)} \rightarrow 0\) as \(k \rightarrow \infty\),
✦
\(A^k v^{(0)}\) = \(A^k( \alpha_1 x_1\) + \(\alpha_2 x_2\) + \(\ldots\) + \(\alpha_n x_n)\)
✦
\(A^k v^{(0)}\) = \(\lambda^k_1 \alpha_1 x_1\) + \(\lambda^k_2 \alpha_2 x_2\) + \(\ldots\) + \(\lambda^k_n \alpha_n x_n\)
✦
\(A^k v^{(0)}\) = \(\lambda^k_1 \Big( \alpha_1 x_1 \) + \(\left(\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}\right)^k \alpha_2 x_2\) + \(\ldots\) + \(\left(\frac{\lambda_n}{\lambda_1}\right)^k \alpha_n x_n \Big) \)
✦
\(A^k v^{(0)}\) = \(\lambda^k_1 \Big( \alpha_1 x_1 \) + \(\epsilon^{(k)} \big)\)
Suppose \(\phi\) is a linear functional with \(\phi(x_1) \neq 0\). Let us now consider the following ratio,
✦
\(r_k\) \(\equiv\) \(\phi(v^{(k+1)})\) \(\phi(v^{(k)})\)
✦
\(r_k\) \(\equiv\) \(\lambda_1 \Big(\) \(\phi(\alpha_1 x_1)\) + \(\phi(\epsilon^{(k+1)})\) \(\phi(\alpha_1 x_1)\) + \(\phi(\epsilon^{(k)})\) \(\Big)\) \(\rightarrow\) \(\lambda_1\)
We will use \(\phi(x) = \|x\|_{\infty}\). Also, note that as \(k \rightarrow \infty\) the direction of \(v^{(k)}\) aligns with \(x_1\). Therefore, \(v^{(k)}\) can be considered as the corresponding eigenvector of the dominant eigenvalue. We also normalize the vector \(v^{(k)}\) after each iteration to prevent the vector being multiplied from growing indefinitely as the iterations progress.
The final method then can be written as the following, \(k = 1,2,\ldots\),
✦ Power Method
✦
\(y^{(k)}\) = \(A v^{(k-1)}\)
✦ Power Method
✦
\(\lambda^{(k)}\) = \(\|y^{(k)}\|_{\infty}\)
✦ Power Method
✦
\(v^{(k)}\) = \(y^{(k)}\) \(\|y^{(k)}\|_{\infty}\)
✦ Example 1 approximating the dominant eigenvalue of the matrix
Problem

Use the Power Method with the Infinity Norm to approximate the dominant eigenvalue of the matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 2\\ 1 & 4\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

with the initial guess

\[ v^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]
Solution

We iteratively compute \(y^{(k)} = Av^{(k - 1)}\), find the row norm, and then normalize.

Iteration 1:

\[ y^{(1)} = Av^{(0)} \] \[ y^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 2\\ 1 & 4\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ y^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 9\\ 5\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \lambda^{(1)} = \|y^{(1)}\|_{\infty} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(1)} = 9 \] \[ v^{(1)} = \frac{y^{(1)}}{\lambda^{(1)}} \\[10pt] v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ \frac{5}{9}\\ \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0.5555\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 2:

\[ y^{(2)} = Av^{(1)} \] \[ y^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 2\\ 1 & 4\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ \frac{5}{9}\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ y^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{73}{9}\\ \frac{29}{9}\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \lambda^{(2)} = \|y^{(2)}\|_{\infty} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(2)} = \frac{73}{9} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(2)} \approx 8.11 \] \[ v^{(2)} = \frac{y^{(2)}}{\lambda^{(2)}} \\[10pt] v^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ \frac{29}{73}\\ \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0.3972\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 3:

\[ y^{(3)} = Av^{(2)} \] \[ y^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 2\\ 1 & 4\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ \frac{29}{73}\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ y^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{569}{73}\\ \frac{189}{73}\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \lambda^{(3)} = \|y^{(3)}\|_{\infty} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(3)} = \frac{569}{73} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(3)} \approx 7.79 \] \[ v^{(3)} = \frac{y^{(3)}}{\lambda^{(3)}} \] \[ v^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ \frac{189}{569}\\ \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0.3321\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Eigenvalues of A:

\[ |A - \lambda I| = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 2\\ 1 & 4\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda & 0\\ 0 & \lambda\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 7-\lambda & 2\\ 1 & 4-\lambda\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ (7-\lambda)(4-\lambda) - 2 = 0 \] \[ 28 - 7\lambda - 4\lambda + \lambda^2 - 2 = 0 \] \[ \lambda^2 - 11\lambda + 26 = 0 \] \[ \lambda_1 = 7.5615 \quad \lambda_2 = 3.4384 \]

\(\lambda_1 = 7.5615\) is dominant eigenvalue.
Eigenvectors of \(A\):
for \(\lambda_1\)

\[ (A - \lambda_1I)x = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 7-7.5615 & 2\\ 1 & 4-7.5615\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} -0.5615 & 2\\ 1 & -3.5615\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ -0.5615x_1 + 2x_2 = 0 \\[10pt] 1x_1 - 3.5615x_2 = 0 \] \[ x_1 = \frac{2}{0.5615}x_2 \] \[ x_1 = 3.5615 x_2 \]

Let \(x_2 = 1\)

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 3.5615\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Normalizing

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0.2807\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Now, for \(\lambda_2\)

\[ (A - \lambda_2I)x = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 7-3.4384 & 2\\ 1 & 4-3.4384\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 3.5616 & 2\\ 1 & 0.5616\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ 3.5616x_1 + 2x_2 = 0 \\[10pt] 1x_1 + 0.5615x_2 = 0 \] \[ x_1 = -0.5615 x_2 \]

Let \(x_2 = 1\)

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -0.5615\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Normalizing

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ -1.7809\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

\(v_1 = [1, 0.2807]^T\) is the dominant eigenvector of \(A\).
The eigenvalue approximations: \(\lambda^{(1)} = 9\), \(\lambda^{(2)} \approx 8.11\), \(\lambda^{(3)} \approx 7.79\) are decreasing toward the true dominant eigenvalue \(7.5615\).
The eigenvector approximations: \(v^{(1)} = [1, 0.5555]^T\), \(v^{(2)} = [1, 0.3972]^T\), \(v^{(3)} = [1, 0.3321]^T\) are converging to the true dominant eigenvector (proportional to \([1, 0.2807]^T\)).
After just 3 iterations, we're getting reasonably close to the true values.
Approximate dominant eigenvalue after 3 iterations:

\[ \lambda_1 \approx 7.79 \]

Approximate dominant eigenvector:

\[ v_1 \approx \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 0.3321\\ \end{bmatrix} \]
✦ Example 2 approximating the dominant eigenvalue of the matrix
Problem

Use the Power Method with the Infinity Norm to approximate the dominant eigenvalue of the matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

with the initial guess

\[ v^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Perform three iterations and report the approximate dominant eigenvalue.

Solution

We iteratively compute \(y^{(k)} = Av^{(k - 1)}\), find the row norm, and then normalize.

Iteration 1:

\[ y^{(1)} = Av^{(0)} \] \[ y^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ y^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 7\\ 7\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \lambda^{(1)} = \|y^{(1)}\|_{\infty} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(1)} = 7 \] \[ v^{(1)} = \frac{y^{(1)}}{\lambda^{(1)}} \\[10pt] v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 2:

\[ y^{(2)} = Av^{(1)} \] \[ y^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ y^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 7\\ 7\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \lambda^{(2)} = \|y^{(2)}\|_{\infty} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(2)} = 7 \] \[ v^{(2)} = \frac{y^{(2)}}{\lambda^{(2)}} \\[10pt] v^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 3:

\[ y^{(3)} = Av^{(2)} \] \[ y^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ y^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 7\\ 7\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \lambda^{(3)} = \|y^{(3)}\|_{\infty} \\[10pt] \lambda^{(3)} = 7 \] \[ v^{(3)} = \frac{y^{(3)}}{\lambda^{(3)}} \] \[ v^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Eigenvalues of A:

\[ |A - \lambda I| = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3\\ 2 & 5\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda & 0\\ 0 & \lambda\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 4-\lambda & 3\\ 2 & 5-\lambda\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ (4-\lambda)(5-\lambda) - 6 = 0 \] \[ 20 - 4\lambda - 5\lambda + \lambda^2 - 6 = 0 \] \[ \lambda^2 - 9\lambda + 14 = 0 \] \[ \lambda_1 = 7 \quad \lambda_2 = 2 \]

\(\lambda_1 = 7\) is dominant eigenvalue.
Eigenvectors of \(A\):
for \(\lambda_1\)

\[ (A - \lambda_1I)x = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 4-7 & 3\\ 2 & 5-7\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} -3 & 3\\ 2 & -2\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ -3x_1 + 3x_2 = 0 \\[10pt] 2x_1 - 2x_2 = 0 \] \[ x_1 = x_2 \]

Let \(x_2 = 1\)

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

Now, for \(\lambda_2\)

\[ (A - \lambda_2I)x = 0 \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 4-2 & 3\\ 2 & 5-2\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3\\ 2 & 3\\ \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1\\ x_2\\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0\\ 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \] \[ 2x_1 + 3x_2 = 0 \] \[ x_1 = -\frac{3}{2}x_2 \]

Let \(x_2 = 2\)

\[ v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -3\\ 2\\ \end{bmatrix} \]

\(v_1 = [1, 1]^T\) is the dominant eigenvector of \(A\).
The eigenvalue approximations: \(\lambda^{(1)} = 7\), \(\lambda^{(2)} = 7\), \(\lambda^{(3)} = 7\) are all exactly equal to the true dominant eigenvalue \(7\) from the very first iteration.
The eigenvector approximations: \(v^{(1)} = [1, 1]^T\), \(v^{(2)} = [1, 1]^T\), \(v^{(3)} = [1, 1]^T\) are all exactly equal to the true dominant eigenvector \([1, 1]^T\).
This is because the initial guess \(v^{(0)} = [1, 1]^T\) happens to be exactly the dominant eigenvector, so the Power Method converged immediately in just one iteration.
Approximate dominant eigenvalue after 3 iterations:

\[ \lambda_1 \approx 7 \]

Approximate dominant eigenvector:

\[ v_1 \approx \begin{bmatrix} 1\\ 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \]
✦ Example 3 finding the dominant eigenvalue and its corresponding eigenvector
Problem

Consider the following \(3 \times 3\) matrix:

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 & 0\\ 1 & 2 & 1\\ 0 & 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \]

Use the power method to find the dominant eigenvalue and its corresponding eigenvector. With initial guess

\[ v^{(0)} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
Solution

Iteration 1:

\[ w^{(1)} = A v^{(0)} \\[10pt] \] \[ w^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 & 0\\ 1 & 2 & 1\\ 0 & 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ w^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \alpha^{(1)} = \|w^{(1)}\|_{\infty}\\[10pt] \alpha^{(1)} = \max(4,4,5) = 5 \] \[ \lambda^{(1)} = 5 \] \[ v^{(1)} = \frac{1}{5} \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ 4 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \\[10pt] v^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.8 \\ 0.8 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 2:

\[ w^{(2)} = A v^{(1)} \] \[ w^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 3.2 \\ 3.4 \\ 4.8 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \alpha^{(2)} = \|w^{(2)}\|_{\infty}\\[10pt] \alpha^{(2)} = \max(3.2,3.4,4.8) = 4.8 \] \[ \lambda^{(2)} = 4.8 \] \[ v^{(2)} = \frac{1}{4.8} \begin{bmatrix} 3.2 \\ 3.4 \\ 4.8 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ v^{(2)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6667 \\ 0.7083 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Iteration 3:

\[ w^{(3)} = A v^{(2)} \] \[ w^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 2.7084 \\ 3.0833 \\ 4.7083 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ \alpha^{(3)} = \|w^{(3)}\|_{\infty}\\[10pt] \alpha^{(3)} = \max(2.7084, 3.0833, 4.7083) = 4.7083 \] \[ \lambda^{(3)} = 4.7083 \] \[ v^{(3)} = \frac{1}{4.7083} \begin{bmatrix} 2.7084 \\ 3.0833 \\ 4.7083 \end{bmatrix} \] \[ v^{(3)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0.5753 \\ 0.6550 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]

Result after 3 iterations:

\[ \lambda_{\max} \approx 4.7083 \] \[ v_{\max} \approx \begin{bmatrix} 0.575 \\ 0.655 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \]
